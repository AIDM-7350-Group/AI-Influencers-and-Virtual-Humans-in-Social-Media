RESEARCH REVIEW

Academic insights and empirical findings on virtual humans and AI influencers.

BACKGROUND

Research Background

Virtual influencers have gradually become a new type of participant in influencer marketing during the quick development of social media and the digital content. They post content, interact with fans, and engage in brand collaborations just like real people. The rise of virtual influencers also reflects a phenomenon that brand prefer to use digital characters that can be designed, controlled, and operated on a sustained basis than real celebrities (Jhawar et al., 2023).

It is defined that virtual influencers are created by AI and computer-generated imagery. Brands are increasingly adopting them, primarily due to their scalability, adaptability, and high brand safety. Compared to real-life influencers, they are easier to manage across platforms and can reduce the risks, making them attractive (Audrezet et al., 2025).

In recent years, this topic has rapidly emerged as a hot academic topic, as it stands at the intersection of AI, social media, and digital marketing. Related research has moved beyond simply examining what virtual influencers are to further exploring how they influence consumers, brand communication, and digital culture, and is gradually entering broader social and governance discussions (Pujadas-Gómez et al., 2025).

Authenticity and Trust

Why can artificial influencers still feel believable?

The fact that users are clearly aware virtual influencers are not real people, but still choose to follow them, interact with them, and even purchase the products they recommend makes they are special. Therefore, although virtual influencers are artificially created, they can still be perceived as authentic social roles, which exemplifies their fake but real contradiction.

In AI influencer marketing, source credibility and authenticity may affect the effective of virtual influencers. This suggests that users make judgments based on whether the influencer’s image, expressions, and recommendations are credible (Kim & Wang, 2024).

The machine heuristic suggests that some users perceive AI-generated content as an objective and stable characteristic. So, in certain situations, virtual influencers may even be perceived as more authentic than real people. At the same time, the perception of authenticity enhances user trust, and trust further influences purchase willingness (Lee et al., 2025).

Transparency is also a crucial factor in building trust. It has been noted that authenticity, ethics, and transparency are core issues in virtual influencer research. If brands or platforms fail to clearly disclose their virtual identities, this may lead to manipulation, misrepresentation, and decrease trust. In contrast, appropriate disclosure helps strengthen users’ sense of trust (Khalfallah & Keller, 2025).

Marketing Effectiveness

When do virtual influencers work best for brands?

Virtual influencers cannot be simply judged as effective or ineffective. Their suitability depends on the specific context. The advantages of virtual influencers do not necessarily from their resemblance to real people but may from their novel and futuristic communication characteristics. Thus, when brands seek to highlight innovation and a sense of technology, virtual influencers may be more suitable compare with real-life influencers (Volles et al., 2024).

In addition to communication effectiveness, cost, risk, and controllability will be considered when brands making actual selections. Virtual influencers are typically easier to control in terms of image and are less influenced by personal controversies, making them more suitable for companies that prioritize brand safety, content consistency, and long-term image management (Hwang et al., 2024).

Parasocial Relationships and Engagement

Why do users feel emotionally connected to virtual influencers?

Although virtual influencers are not real people, users may still feel a sense of familiarity, companionship, and affection toward them.

By sharing content and interactive behaviors, virtual influencers can improve users’ engagement, which further influences their willingness to follow a brand. Engagement and reposting are key indicators of this relationship, suggesting that users’ reactions to virtual influencers go beyond simple amusement and manifest such as liking, interacting, and sharing. At the same time, if a virtual influencer appears overly lifelike but lacks naturalness, it may trigger the uncanny valley effect, then reducing user acceptance (Guo et al., 2025).

Users follow virtual influencers not only because of the characters themselves but also the community that forms around them. Interactions among followers influence users’ virtual interaction and attachment to virtual influencers, indicating that emotional attachment increasing not only from the virtual influencers themselves but also from the community atmosphere and fan interactions (Bang & Han, 2025).

Ethical issues and regulation

What ethical and regulatory issues are raised by virtual influencers?

While virtual influencers can boost engagement, the lack of authenticity, transparency, and the potential for misinformation will always impact user trust. If brands fail to clearly disclose their virtual status, users may feel manipulated or misled (Khalfallah and Keller, 2025).

This also raises the issue of manipulation. Since virtual influencers can act as real people to create content, express emotions, and establish personas in ways that closely mimic real people, they may influence users’ judgment by appearing highly authentic. Therefore, this is not just a matter of communication methods but also involves consumer protection.

China has proposed a draft regulation on digital avatars. When using virtual influencers, requiring clear labeling of virtual content. If it is lacking the agreement of users it is prohibited to use others’ data to create digital. The use of digital avatars to circumvent identity verification is banned, and prohibiting the provision of simulated intimate relationship services to minors that could lead to dependency. This indicates that the governance of digital avatars is now closely linked to identity rights, the protection of minors, and platform safety responsibilities (Reuters, 2026).
